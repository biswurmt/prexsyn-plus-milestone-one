Research Report on

Rule-based and learned molecular modification methods for property-preserving analog generation
Full search query: I want to find well-established, computational molecular modification methods (both learned and rule-based) that take a
single, drug-like small-molecule input—such as a PrexSyn-generated compound from Enamine REAL space—and generate a set of structurally
related variants, spanning local to global modifications, that are suitable for post hoc filtering for synthesizability and approximate conservation
of a user-defined property specification (e.g., activity/ADMET); the methods should include at least one representative of each of the following
categories: scaffold-constrained decoration/R-group generation, latent-space molecular editing (encode–perturb–decode), matched molecular
pair or bioisosteric transformations, fragment-based recombination or retrosynthetic decomposition/reassembly, and ring system or heterocycle
replacement, and should have publicly available implementations and demonstrated use on drug-like molecules

Summary
The literature reveals a mature but fragmented landscape of methods that do satisfy your “single drug-like molecule in ’ analog set out”
requirement across all requested categories—scaffold decoration, latent-space editing, MMP/bioisosteres, fragment/reaction-based
recombination, and ring/heterocycle replacement—with several practically usable, open implementations (notably CReM [1], mmpdb
[2], MMPtrained Transformer models [3], Modof [4], JTVAE/related VAEs [5,7], scaffold decorators [6,9,19,20,36], fragmentRL
and reaction-based frameworks [8,10,11,15,17,24,38,49]) but no single system yet provides an integrated, synthesisaligned,
propertyaware “localtoglobal” editor tailored to spaces like Enamine REAL.

Overall Picture: What Exists Today and How It Matches Your Goal
Core conclusion:You can cover your full set of operator types using existing, well-established methods, but you will need to
compose them into a workflow:
• Scaffoldconstrained decoration / Rgroup generation is well served by SMILES and graph scaffold decorators (ArúsPous et
al. [6], DeepScaffold and Lim’s scaffold models [19,20,43], Sc2Mol [9], SAMOA [36], LibINVENT [35], EMPIRE [31], MolDecor
[33]) that start from an explicit scaffold (often extracted from the lead) and sample decorations, often with property conditioning
or RL.
• Latentspace encode–perturb–decode is provided by VAEs and related latent models (GómezBombarelli VAE [7], JTVAE [5],
Modof [4], CLaSMO [12], InVirtuoGen [13]) that encode a seed molecule, perturb its latent representation (via gradients or
BO), and decode structural neighbors with property guidance.
• MMP/bioisosteric transformations are operationalized by mmpdb [2], He et al.’s MMPtrained SMILES transformers [3], MECo
[39], and MMPderived edit vocabularies in newer editors [28,46], giving local, empirically propertyaligned edits.
• Fragmentbased recombination / retrosynthetic reassembly is strongly represented by CReM [1], fragmentRL methods
(Ståhl [14], FREED [8], fragRL [24], Gargoyles [10], SHARP [27]), reactiondriven optimizers and enumerators (ReactEA [11],
REACTOR [17], PathFinder [15], DOGS [49], DOTS [50], growing/linking optimizers [32]), and orchestration frameworks like
Deriver [38].
• Ring system / heterocycle replacement is covered as a special case of fragment/ring operations (CReM [1], MIMOSA
[18]) and scaffoldbioisostere search tools like ScaffoldKeys [16], plus the core enumeration / scaffold hopping capabilities in
PathFinder [15], SHARP [27], and related systems.
However:
• No single public tool takes a PrexSynstyle input and, in one step, returns a hierarchically structured analog set spanning
MMPsmall, Rgroup decoration, ring/core swaps, and full scaffold hops under explicit Enamine REAL–aligned synthesis
constraints.
• There is a clear practical path to such a system by combining:(i) a local editor (e.g., CReM [1], mmpdb/He et al. [2,3]),(ii)
scaffold/Rgroup decorators (e.g., [6,19,20,35,36]),(iii) reaction/reagentdriven generators aligned to REALlike chemistries
(PathFinder [15], ReactEA [11], REACTOR [17], DOGS [49], DOTS [50], growing/linking optimizers [32]), and(iv) an outer
orchestration/search layer (Deriver [38], or your own agent framework) that handles scoring and filtering for synthesizability
and ADMET.
Below, I summarize what is available in each category with an eye to: (1) operational interface (singlemolecule input), (2) control
over local ’ global edits, (3) synthesizability alignment, and (4) suitability as components in your desired workflow.

LatentSpace Encode–Perturb–Decode Editing
Foundational latent models
• GómezBombarelli VAE [7]
• Encodes a molecule to a continuous vector; decoding after small perturbations yields variants; gradients of a learned
property predictor can be used for property optimization.
• Good for smooth, global property adjustments, but similarity to the seed is controlled only by perturbation magnitude,
not structural constraints.
• JTVAE [5]
• Latent space over a junction tree of substructures + full graph; latent moves translate into substructurelevel changes.
• Demonstrated property-guided optimization from a seed, with chemically valid, structurally related analogs [5].
• Public implementation and widely used benchmarks make it a practical starting point.

Edit-focused latent models
• Modof / Modofpipe [4]
• Learns the difference between reactant/product at one disconnection site, operating explicitly at fragment level.
• Modofpipe chains multiple steps for larger structural changes; property objectives include penalized logP, QED, DRD2,
with SA penalty [4].
• Excellent match to your need for singlefragment and multi-fragment edits with property guidance, though synthesizability is only indirectly handled via SA.
• CLaSMO [12]
• Conditional VAE trained to generate substructures attached at specific scaffold environments; combined with latentspace
Bayesian optimization under similarity constraints.
• Explicitly formulated as “given scaffold S, generate S2 with small modifications and better properties”(QED, docking)
[12].
• A good candidate when you need small, environmentspecific structure tweaks with BO-driven sample efficiency.
• InVirtuoGen [13]
• Discrete flow over fragmented SMILES (rBRICS); supports fragment-constrained design and lead optimization.
• Uses Proximal Property Optimization and a quality constraint (QED e 0.6, SA d 4) [13].
• Bridges latent flows with fragment-level control, giving you fragment-wise yet property-tuned moves.

Relevance to your goal
• Latent models are best at smoothing property landscapes and exploring beyond very local edits, especially for multi-objective optimization.
• They do not enforce synthesis but can be wrapped with synthesizability and similarity filters, or combined with fragment/reaction-based generators via frameworks like Deriver [38].
• For your purposes, Modof [4], CLaSMO [12], JTVAE [5], and InVirtuoGen [13] are the most directly useful, providing
encode–perturb–decode pipelines that accept a seed and return optimized analogs at controllable structural distances.

MMP / Bioisosteric Transformations
Rule-based infrastructure
• mmpdb [2]
• Open-source platform to mine MMPs and apply transformation rules to new molecules.
• Interface: Given a molecule, identify sites where MMP rules apply, and enumerate analogs by replacing “from” fragments
with “to” fragments.
• Properties: Rules can be annotated by empirical property deltas, allowing you to pick transformations likely to increase/decrease e.g. logD, pKa.
• Use: A solid rule-based local editor for property-informed MMP edits, fully compatible with downstream filtering.
• Medicinal-chemistry rule sets (Segall, StarDrop; Drug Guru) [48,51]
• ~200 SMIRKS transformation rules grouped into ring additions/removals, linker changes, etc.
• Used historically within proprietary tools, but conceptually align with an MMP/bioisosteric operator class.

Learned MMP translators and editors
• He et al. 2020 (MMPtrained Transformers) [3]
• Models learn SMILES’SMILES translation from MMP-derived pairs, taking as input a molecule + desired property change
vector (e.g., l”ogD, ”solubility, ”clearance).
• Output analogs tend to be small, chemistintuitive edits and can address multi-property optimization [3].
• Public code and data, so a very attractive choice for datadriven local property tuning.
• MECo (“Coder as Editor”) [39] and MolEditRL [46]
• MECo trains an LLM to emit Python/SMARTS code edits from realistic edit corpora combining MMPs and reactionderived
edits [39].
• MolEditRL learns instructionaligned edits via a discrete diffusion + RL framework, trained partly on MMP-derived
examples [46].

Fragment-Based Recombination and Retrosynthetic Decomposition/Reassembly
Chemically reasonable fragment edits with open tools
• CReM [1]
• Fragment substitutions under context constraints; uses a fragment database mined from real molecules.
• Interface: Given a molecule, perform “mutate/grow/link” operations that swap fragments in specific local contexts.
• Local vs global: You can tune the context radius and fragment size to move from very local changes (Rgroup tweaks) to
more substantial modifications (ring/core changes).
• Synthesizability: Uses only fragments observed in known compounds and enforces valence/context compatibility; no
explicit routes but high plausibility.
• Use: A very strong general-purpose local editor; widely used and easy to integrate into custom pipelines.
• Deriver [38]
• Modular Python framework that wraps generators like CReM and search strategies; separates generation, scoring, and
exploration.
• Provides evolutionary and Monte Carlo search and supports plugin property filters and medicinalchemistry rules.
• Use: Ideal as a top-level orchestration layer that can combine CReM, latent models, and reaction-based generators
under a unified API.
• Gargoyles [10]
• Open-source graphbased RL; starts from a given molecule and does fragment-wise then atom-wise search.
• Optimizes QED; DRD2 case study; uses AIZynthFinder to assess routes [10].
• Use: A practical automated local fragment editor with builtin property optimization and retrosynthesis checking.

Reaction- and reagent-driven frameworks
• PathFinder [15]
• Retrosynthesis ’ enumerative Rgroup/core variation via reaction templates and catalog building blocks; integrated with
FEP + active learning.
• Centered on designing synthetically accessible analogs; applied to optimize CDK2 inhibitors [15].
• Use: A powerful synthesis-aligned analog enumerator; ideal template if you want to align to Enamine REAL (by plugging
its reactions/building blocks).
• ReactEA [11], REACTOR [17], growing/linking optimizers [32]
• ReactEA: evolutionary search over 22,949 biochemical reaction rules; ensures synthetic feasibility and tracks routes [11].
• REACTOR: RL agent whose actions are reaction templates, generating molecules accompanied by synthetic routes
[17].
• Growing/linking optimizers: reaction-based assembly with controllable building-block and reaction sets; shown to produce
more synthesizable molecules than text-based generators [32].
• Use: Strong candidates for reaction-space optimization around a lead, especially once adapted to an Enamine REAL
reaction set.
• DOGS [49], DOTS [50]
• DOGS: reaction-driven de novo design from building blocks; each molecule has at least one synthetic route; supports
scaffold hopping [49].
• DOTS: integrated fragment-growing with encoded one-step reactions, automated synthesis and testing [50].

Ring System and Heterocycle Replacement
Direct ring-editing operators
• CReM [1] and MIMOSA [18]
• CReM’s fragment set includes ring systems; context-controlled replacement naturally covers heterocycle swaps, ring
expansions/contractions [1].
• MIMOSA’s add/replace/delete at the single-ring level was explicitly shown to perform ring replacements while controlling
similarity and properties [18].
• Use: These give you local ring-level operators integrated within more general fragment edit frameworks.

Scaffold bioisosteres and scaffold hopping
• ScaffoldKeys (Ertl) [16]
• A descriptor-based similarity method to identify bioisosteric scaffolds; implemented as a free web tool.
• Very suitable for suggesting alternative cores with similar topological/physicochemical features.
• Use: As a core-replacement idea generator, which can then be combined with scaffold decorators and fragment editors.
• Core enumeration and scaffold hopping in reaction-based tools
• PathFinder’s core enumeration [15], SHARP’s scaffold hopping task [27], and Makkawi’s retroforward analog design [41]
all support ring/core replacements tied to synthetic routes or docking optimization.
• Use: For systematic core swaps under synthetic control, integrated with property scoring.

Relevance to your goal
• For ring/heterocycle replacement you can combine:
• ScaffoldKeys [16] to suggest alternative cores.
• CReM/MIMOSA [1,18] to instantiate those replacements in specific molecules.
• Reaction-based engines [15,11,17,41] to enumerate synthetically reachable analogs around new cores.

1. Scaffoldconstrained decoration / Rgroup generation
Key representatives: SMILES scaffold decorator [6], DeepScaffold [19], Lim scaffoldbased GGNN [20,43], LibINVENT/SAMOA
[35,36,45], Sc2Mol [9], CLaSMO [12], EMPIRE [31], MolDecor [33], MoLeR [22].
What distinguishes these methods
• How the scaffold is specified and enforced
• Explicit SMILES with attachment points. ArúsPous et al. [6], LibINVENT [35], GPMoLFormer’s decoration mode
[26], MolDecor [33] take SMILES scaffolds with explicit “*” points and generate decorations; enforcement is trivial by
construction (they append tokens around the fixed scaffold substring).
• Graphlevel scaffold graphs. DeepScaffold [19] and Lim et al. [20,43] represent the scaffold as a graph and generate
supergraphs conditioned on it, guaranteeing inclusion by construction. This is generally more robust to atom ordering and
supports more complex topologies.
• Scaffold + environment specification. DeepScaffold allows multiple scaffold notions (Bemis–Murcko, cyclic skeletons,
sidechain/pharmacophore constraints) [19]; CLaSMO focuses on Bemis–Murcko scaffolds with atomenvironment–specific
substructure generators [12].
• Implicit scaffolds via masking. Delete [28] and SHARP [27] use masking or hierarchical action spaces to perform
fragment growing, linker design, scaffold hopping, and sidechain decoration; here “scaffoldconstrained” tasks are defined
by which parts are frozen vs. editable.
• Control of modification locality and combinatorial explosion
• Most decorators (SMILESbased [6,35,36,26], Sc2Mol [9]) handle multiattachmentpoint decoration, but controlling the
combinatorial explosion is done by:
• Limiting decorator library size (MolDecor’s propertyspecific decorator library [33]).
• Samplingbased generation rather than full enumeration [6,35,36,9].
• RL or BO over decorations (SAMOA [36], REINVENT/LibINVENT [35,45], CLaSMO [12]).
• EMPIRE [31] combines a deep generator with a buildingblockbased generator to exhaustively enumerate scaffoldretained
molecules in userdefined chemical spaces (e.g., BCP, cubane, boroncontaining scaffolds) while maintaining tractability
[31].
• Property guidance
• Direct multiobjective conditioning: Lim et al. [20,43] and DeepScaffold [19] condition generation on property vectors,
demonstrating joint control (e.g., EGFR inhibitors [20], DRD2 docking scores [19]).
• Posthoc scoring or RL: SAMOA [36] and LibINVENT/REINVENT workflows [35,45] use RL hillclimbing where the reward
is a composite QSAR/docking/physchem score; CLaSMO uses latentspace BO under scaffold/similarity constraints [12].
• Datasetlevel bias: ArúsPous et al. [6] show that training on DRD2 actives leads to decorated molecules predicted active,
effectively embedding the property profile into the learned decoration distribution.
• Synthesizability alignment
• Reactionaware slicing and buildingblock alignment. ArúsPous et al. create scaffold–decoration pairs using syntheticchemistry constraints and check generated decorations against ZINC InStock fragments [6]. LibINVENT is designed to
decorates with reagents from vendorlike libraries [35].

2. Latentspace encode–perturb–decode editing
Key representatives: GómezBombarelli VAE [7], JTVAE [5], Modof [4], Sc2Mol [9], CLaSMO [12], InVirtuoGen [13], Barshatski &
Radinsky [25], GAFSE [40], ChemSpacE [37].
How these models realize “local” vs “global” edits
• Continuous latent geometry:
• GómezBombarelli et al. [7] and JTVAE [5] show that small perturbations in latent space often correspond to chemically
reasonable variations, while larger steps effect scaffold hops or globally different chemotypes.
• CLaSMO [12] uses latentspace BO while explicitly constraining similarity (S ’ S2) and focusing changes on scaffold bonding
points, making it suited to small, targeted optimization rather than large scaffold hops.
• ChemSpacE [37] (details not in excerpt) and GAFSEMO [40] focus on interpretable directions in latent space, e.g., moving
along axes associated with increasing QED or other properties.
• Differencebased latent modeling (edit rather than reconstruct)
• Modof learns to encode the difference between reactant and product at a disconnection site [4], making it naturally suited
to fragmentlevel edits around a single cut. The Modofpipe variants chain such edits, allowing an explicit localtoglobal
continuum of modifications [4].
• Barshatski & Radinsky [25] perform unpaired moleculetomolecule translation with double cycle constraints, which implicitly
aligns latent representations of molecules and their optimized counterparts, though the paper emphasizes property
optimization tasks rather than explicit locality control.
• Fragment/discrete latent spaces
• InVirtuoGen [13] replaces continuous vectors with discrete flows on fragmented SMILES, enabling controlled fragment
insertions/replacements while still allowing propertyguided optimization via a PMOstyle objective (QED and SA) [13].
• Sc2Mol [9] uses a CVAE to generate coarse scaffolds (latent) and then a transformer to decorate them, effectively
combining a latent scaffold generator with a conditional decorator.
Property conditioning strategies
• Gradientbased optimization in latent space (GómezBombarelli [7]) is well suited to smooth properties with reasonably
accurate predictors.
• Bayesian optimization in latent space (JTVAE [5], CLaSMO [12]) provides sampleefficient search under similarity constraints
and is more robust to multimodal landscapes.
• Latentspace regularization toward property alignment: GAFSE uses propertyguided graph autoencoders and adversarial
learning [40]; MoLeR adds property losses to encourage propertysmooth latent neighborhoods [22].
Synthesizability considerations
• Most latent models enforce chemical validity but not synthetic accessibility by construction. Typical mitigations are:
• Penalized logP (including SA component) as optimization target (Modof [4], MIMOSA [18]).
• SA score/QED used in composite quality metrics (InVirtuoGen’s “Quality” criterion: QED e 0.6, SA d 4) [13].
• Posthoc syntheticroute evaluation (e.g., Gargoyles uses AIZynthFinder [10]; similar strategies can be combined with
JTVAE/VAEs via Deriver [38]).
Implications
• Latent editing is powerful for continuous property tuning and can produce both local and global analogs from a seed.
• For practically synthesizable analogs, latent methods require explicit coupling to fragment/reactionbased filters or retrosynthesizers (e.g., using Deriver [38] as an outer loop over JTVAE or Modof).

3. Matched molecular pairs (MMPs) and bioisosteric transformations
Key representatives: mmpdb [2], He et al. [3], MECo [39], Delete’s MMPbased masking [28], medicinalchemistry rule sets [48,51].
Rulebased vs learned transform application
• Rulebased MMP toolkit (mmpdb):
• Extracts MMP rules from large datasets and supports applying these rules to new molecules [2].
• Offers both contextfree and contextspecific transformations (depending on how rules are mined).
• Does not directly integrate property predictors, but transforms can be grouped by empirically observed property deltas,
enabling rule selection based on desired property changes.
• Learned MMPstyle translation (He et al.):
• Models map (starting molecule, desired property change ”) ’optimized analog [3].
• Training on mined MMPs implicitly captures chemists’ local intuition and typical property shifts, allowing multiproperty
control for logD, solubility, clearance [3].
• Compared to rulebased application, this can generalize beyond explicit rules while remaining largely local in practice.
• Instructionaligned editing (MECo):
• Builds a realistic edit corpus from USPTOderived reaction pairs and ChEMBLderived MMPs [39].
• A cascaded LLM learns to map (molecule, naturallanguage edit intention) ’ Python code implementing SMARTSbased
edits [39].
• High edit reproduction accuracy (>98% on heldout edits) suggests faithful application of a broad set of humaninterpretable transformations [39].
• MMPinformed decomposition for other models
• Delete uses MMPbased double cuts for linker design and single cuts for fragment elaboration [28], integrating MMP
notions into graphmasking tasks.
• Medicinalchemistry transformation sets (StarDrop/Segall et al. [48,51]) encode ~200 SMIRKSbased rules for ring addition/modification/removal, linker modification, etc., used in commercial workflows.
Property conservation & transferability
• MMPbased operations are naturally suited to local property modification around a lead:
• He et al. [3] demonstrate tripleproperty optimization with small, intuitive edits.
• MECo [39] shows high structure–intention consistency and increased optimization success rates vs SMILES generators.
Synthesizability
• Rulebased transforms (Segall et al. [48,51]) and commercial tools (Drug Guru, StarDrop) often encode medicinalchemistryplausible reactions, but synthesizability is not guaranteed for all transform combinations [48].
• MMPDB and He et al. [3] do not explicitly incorporate synthetic routes; however, restricting rules to reactionderived MMPs
(as in Delete’s decomposition [28]) can improve plausibility.
Implications
• For finegrained, propertypreserving edits (e.g., optimizing logD, clearance, solubility without leaving a scaffold series),
MMPbased methods like mmpdb [2], He et al. [3], and MECo [39] are natural candidates.
• Tools like mmpdb are mature, scalable, and opensource, well suited as building blocks in a larger analoggeneration workflow.

4. Fragmentbased recombination and retrosynthetic decomposition/reassembly

Knowledge source and action space

• CReM builds a fragment database (e.g., from PAINSfiltered ChEMBL) and uses contextradius constraints for “chemically

and explicit route tracing [11].
• REACTOR [17], DOGS [49], PathFinder [15], DOTS [50], and the growing/linking optimizers [32] operate directly in
reaction space, assembling products from vendor building blocks via encoded reactions.

Timeline
Chronological Overview and Major Milestones
Pre–Deep Learning Era (2011–2015): Rule-Based Transformations and Reaction-Driven Design
Medicinal-chemistry transformations and MMP-style thinking
• Segall et al. 2011 formalized medicinal chemistry transformation sets (206 SMIRKS-encoded reactions grouped into
ring/functional-group/linker/terminal-group manipulations) and integrated them into StarDrop for rule-based virtual lead
optimization, with QSAR- and ADME-driven multiparameter optimization [48,51].
• This crystallized the idea that codified transformations (essentially MMP/bioisosteric rules) could be systematically applied
to input leads in silico.
Reaction-driven de novo and analog design
• DOGS (Hartenfeller & Schneider 2012) is an early, highly influential reaction-driven generator [49]:
• Uses 58 formalized reaction rules and >25k commercial building blocks.
• Generates structures and synthetic routes together, optimizing ligand-based similarity to one or more reference
compounds [49].
• Already supports scaffold hopping, bioisosteric replacements, and fragment-based expansion of reference ligands,
anticipating later “synthesis-aware” generators.
Fragment-growing workflows integrated with synthesis
• DOTS (Hoffer et al. 2018), although later chronologically, is conceptually part of this tradition: fragment growing around an
“activated fragment” using encoded one-step reactions and building-block libraries, with automated synthesis and assay in a
closed loop [50].
• This embodies the reaction-templated fragment-growing / R-group enumeration pattern that later deep-learning
models will try to emulate with learned policies.
Key themes established
• Local, rule-based edits (MMP-like, SMIRKS-encoded).
• Reaction- and building-block-driven feasibility (DOGS, DOTS).
• Explicit hit-to-lead / lead-optimization workflows, not just generic “molecular generation”.
These works set up the idea that analog generation is an operator on a given molecule, implemented via either curated
transformations or practical synthetic routes, and that feasibility and property profiles must be considered together.

First Wave of Latent-Space Generators and RL (2016–2018): From Unconstrained Generation to Local Optimization
Continuous latent spaces and encode–perturb–decode
• Gómez-Bombarelli et al. 2016 introduced the now-classic VAE latent space for molecules [7].
• Enabled encode–perturb–decode workflows: encode a molecule, move in latent space (often guided by gradients of a
property predictor), decode analogs [7].
• While mostly demonstrated as global de novo design, it explicitly illustrated local neighborhood exploration from a
single seed.
• JT-VAE (Jin et al. 2018) encoded molecules as a junction tree of substructures plus the full graph, giving a more chemically
meaningful latent space [5].
• Decoding via substructure trees made chemically valid edits and scaffold-level changes much easier.
• Property optimization from a seed molecule and interpolation in latent space were demonstrated, making JTVAE a key
latent-editing milestone.
These works introduced the field’s latent-space viewpoint on “local chemical neighborhoods” and spurred many later encode–perturb–decode optimizers.
RL on SMILES and fragment replacements
• REINVENT / Olivecrona et al. 2017: SMILES RNN Prior fine-tuned via RL (augmented likelihood) [45].

2018–2020: Matched Molecular Pairs, Local Edits, and Scaffold-Conditioned Deep Models
MMP infrastructure and data-driven local transformations
• mmpdb (Dalke et al. 2018) provided an open-source platform for mining and working with matched molecular pairs at scale
[2].
• It catalyzed the use of MMP-derived transformations as mechanizable building blocks.
• He et al. 2020 (“Molecular optimization by capturing chemist’s intuition”) used mined MMPs to train SMILES’SMILES
translation models (Seq2Seq, Transformer, HierG2G) that take a single molecule plus desired property change and output
analogs [3].
• This is a direct realization of the MMP/bioisostere-style, local-edit generator with an explicit single-molecule input
and property-guided output.
• Public code and datasets make this a key milestone for data-driven local transformation learning.
Scaffold-constrained decorators and scaffold-based generators
• Lim et al. 2019 / DeepScaffold & Lim 2019/2020 follow-up: graph-based scaffold-conditioned generators that take a
Bemis–Murcko scaffold and grow full molecules while enforcing scaffold presence and controlling properties [19,20,43].
• Mark the emergence of scaffold-preserving graph generators with property conditioning (e.g., EGFR inhibitors) [20].
• Arús-Pous et al. 2020 (Scaffold decorator): SMILES-based decorator model that takes a scaffold with attachment points
and generates decorations, using a systematic slicing pipeline to create scaffold–decoration pairs [6].
• Demonstrated on DRD2 and ChEMBL subsets; also considered purchasability via ZINC In-Stock [6].
• Establishes the modern scaffold-decoration paradigm that later models and industrial tools (e.g., Lib-INVENT-based
workflows) build upon.
• SAMOA (Langevin et al. 2020): a sampling trick on a SMILES RNN enabling scaffold-constrained generation and RL
optimization without retraining [36].
• Demonstrated scaffold-constrained DRD2 optimization and a lead-optimization case on an MMP-12 series, explicitly
aligning with medicinal-chemistry practice [36].
Fragment- and reaction-based optimization
• CReM (Polishchuk 2020): a chemically reasonable mutation framework using fragment substitution under context constraints [1].
• Provides explicit local, fragment-level edit operators and is open-source, making it a key infrastructure piece.
• Although not inherently property-guided, it is designed to be plugged into optimization workflows.
• Ståhl et al. 2019: fragment-based RL where an actor–critic RNN replaces fragments in lead molecules, biasing toward
structurally similar analogs while optimizing multiple properties [14].
• This anticipates later fragment-RL frameworks like FREED and SHARP.
Latent-space and local edit refinement
• MIMOSA (Fu et al. 2020): graph-based MCMC with learned atom-/ring-level add/replace/delete moves, starting from a single
input molecule and sampling under constraints on similarity and properties [18].
• Offers a principled, multi-constraint local search over graph edits, including ring replacements, even though code
availability is unclear.
• Modof (Chen et al. 2020): explicitly one-fragment modification model, learning a “difference” distribution between reactant/product at a disconnection site [4].
• Chains multiple Modof steps for more global changes (Modof-pipe) and demonstrates single- and multi-property optimization with similarity control [4].

2020–2022: Synthesis-Aware Fragment Models, RL and Retrosynthetic Enumeration
Reaction- and retrosynthesis-based analog generation
• Konze et al. 2019 (PathFinder) built a retrosynthesis- and reaction-based enumeration engine integrated with FEP and
active learning [15].
• Retrosynthetic decomposition ’ R-group/core enumeration ’ FEP ranking ’ active learning closes a loop for CDK2 lead
optimization [15].
• This is a major synthesis-aware, reaction-template-based analog enumeration platform, directly suited to vendor
spaces like Enamine REAL.
• REACTOR (Horwood & Noutahi 2020) frames molecule design as an RL agent applying reaction templates and building
blocks; trajectories begin from building blocks or starting molecules and remain synthetically accessible by construction
[17].
• Includes multi-objective optimization with synthetic-accessibility and drug-likeness scores [17].
• Arai et al. 2018 (virtual compound synthesis system) also used recursive application of virtual synthetic reaction rules to
input compounds, emphasizing structural preservation and property improvement [21].
• Conceptually a precursor to reaction-based RL, though less tied to modern deep models.
These works show a convergence of generative modeling with retrosynthetic logic, moving from heuristic SA scores toward
explicit reaction paths and building-block libraries.
Fragment-based RL and scaffold preservation
• Ståhl et al. 2019 (above) ’ extended by FREED (Yang et al. 2021): a fragment-based RL framework with SAC and prioritized
replay, attaching fragments to a starting molecule and filtering by medicinal chemistry rules, docking and SA [8].
• Explicitly supports a “use existing molecule as seed” mode, with fragment growth constrained by pre-defined attachment rules and docking-based reward [8].
• Sauer et al. 2023 (fragRL): actor–critic fragment RL integrating reaction schemes, reagent databases, and virtual library
encodings, with options like fragment freezing and reagent-based fragment sources [24].
• Provides a more systematic reaction-scheme-based fragmentation and explores building-block–aligned chemical
spaces; code is publicly available [24].
Scaffold-based and fragment-based deep models
• Sc2Mol (Liao et al. 2022): a two-step VAE + Transformer pipeline; generates coarse scaffolds then decorates them [9].
• Supports both de novo generation and taking a scaffold as input for decoration, learning scaffold’candidate relationships without explicit hand-crafted rules [9].
• MoLeR (Maziarz et al. 2021): a motif- and atom-based graph model that extends an input scaffold with learned motifs and
atoms, with strong performance on scaffold-constrained tasks [22].
• Incorporates motif vocabulary learning and an auxiliary property loss to align latent neighborhoods with property
similarity [22].
• EMPIRE (Kaitoh & Yamanishi 2022): scaffold-retained generator combining a deep learning generator and building-block-based generator to create virtual libraries retaining a given scaffold [31].
• Explicitly designed for scaffold-retained exploration in arbitrary chemical space, including unusual skeletons and
elements [31].
Trend: Between ~2019–2022, synthesis-aware, fragment- and reaction-based frameworks mature:
• RL systems (REINVENT-derived, Ståhl, FREED, fragRL) are increasingly combined with reaction schemes and reagent
databases [14,8,24].
• Retrosynthetic engines like PathFinder connect enumeration tightly to physics-based scoring (FEP) and active learning [15].
• Scaffold/motif-based generators (MoLeR, Sc2Mol, EMPIRE) provide controllable scaffold-level operators solidifying the
scaffold-decorate paradigm.

• MolEditRL (Zhuang et al. 2025): two-stage framework combining discrete graph diffusion and RL, conditioned on natural-language editing instructions [46].

• MIMOSA (2020) and related MCMC approaches are conceptually similar precursors, but MolEditRL makes instruction-conditioned editing a central goal.
Scaffold and substructure–focused latent optimizers
• CLaSMO (Boyar et al. 2024): Conditional VAE for substructure generation inserted into a specified scaffold, coupled to

• Explicitly framed as scaffold-constrained, small substructure modifications preserving similarity while optimizing

• CFOM and unpaired translation (Barshatski & Radinsky 2021; Kaminsky et al. 2023): lead optimization via unpaired

• These define a clear core vs. periphery decomposition, learning how to regenerate “chains” (decorations) with desired
properties for a fixed core [23].
Fragment- and flow-based generative refinement
• InVirtuoGen / discrete flows (Kaech et al. 2025): operates on fragmented SMILES (rBRICS) with an explicit fragment-constrained design and lead optimization mode [13].
• Introduces Proximal Property Optimization for fine-tuning the flow model toward property profiles, with constraints on
QED and SA [13].
Ring/scaffold replacement and scaffold-hopping models
• Ertl’s Scaffold Keys and web tool (2021): provides an automated, similarity-based way to identify bioisosteric scaffolds
via Scaffold Keys, with a public web implementation [16].
• While more of an analytical/search tool than a generator, it codifies scaffold-bioisostere relationships that can be
plugged into generative workflows.
• Xu et al. 2022 (stereochemistry-aware scaffold model) builds on Lim’s scaffold-based generator, adding USRCAT-based
3D similarity and pharmacophore matching for scaffold hopping with chirality preservation [44].
• Bridges 2D scaffold-based generation with 3D shape/pharmacophore constraints.
Instruction-/code-driven molecular editing and multi-agent systems
• MECo (Zhu et al. 2025): “coder as editor” approach where a model generates SMARTS + Python code edits from a molecule
and intent, trained on reaction- and MMP-derived edits [39].
• Emphasizes interpretable, auditable transformations and achieves high accuracy in reproducing realistic edits [39].
• Multi-agent platforms (Ünlü et al. 2025): an auditable agent platform where different agents (LLM-based, docking tools,
ranking and critic agents) iteratively edit scaffolds, optimizing docking, QED, SA, and LogP [42].
• Illustrates a shift from single end-to-end models to orchestrated systems where editing tools, property oracles, and
decision logic interact.
• Trio / FRAGPT (Ji et al. 2025): fragment language model + DPO alignment + MCTS, focusing on fragment assembly and
pocket-conditioned planning with QED/SA-guided reward [47].
• Less explicitly “single-input–analog-set” but clearly structured around fragment assembly with synthetic and drug-likeness priors.
Synthesis-driven, Pareto-optimized fragment RL
• SHARP (Kim et al. 2025): fragment-based RL with a hierarchical action space and learned Synthesizability Estimation Model
(SEM) to mask actions [27].
• Explicitly targets Pareto optimization (affinity, synthesizability, drug-likeness), and benchmarks across fragment growing,
linker design, scaffold hopping, side-chain decoration [27].
Trend: 2022–2025 is characterized by:
• Fine-grained, structure-preserving editing paradigms (Gargoyles, MolEditRL, MECo).
• Explicit treatment of instructions and interpretability (natural language, code, multi-agent “medicinal chemist” agents).
• Deeper integration of synthetic feasibility, either via reaction masks (SHARP), retrosynthesis (Gargoyles, PathFinder line),
or building-block-based fragment sources (fragRL, Trio).

Thematic Evolution Across Method Categories
1. Scaffold-Constrained Decoration / R-Group Generation
Early rule-based and interactive tools
• Pre-deep-learning workflows (Segall, StarDrop; DOGS; DOTS) already implemented scaffold-locked R-group enumeration
via reaction templates and curated R-group sets [49,50,51].
Deep learning and SMILES-based decorators (2018–2020)
• Scaffold decorator (Arús-Pous 2020) is a clear early deep-learning example of SMILES-based scaffold ’ decorated analogs
[6].
• SAMOA (2020) and Sc2Mol (2022) broadened this with generic RNN/VAE + sampling strategies, enabling scaffold-constrained RL optimization [36,9].
• DeepScaffold / Lim 2019–2020: graph-based scaffold-to-molecule models with property conditioning [19,20,43].
Property-conditioned decorators and integration with docking/ADMET
• Lib-INVENT-based workflows in Sauer et al. 2022 integrate scaffold decorators into docking + ML scoring pipelines, showing
practical application of scaffold-constrained decoration with multi-parameter scoring [35].
• MolDecor (2025) focuses on property-specific decorator libraries and BERT-like models to learn scaffold–decorator
relationships for property optimization, explicitly claiming synthesizability via fragment sourcing [33].
Summary: Scaffold-decoration has evolved from hand-coded reaction-based R-group enumeration to deep models trained
on scaffold–decoration pairs, now increasingly property-conditioned and integrated with docking/ADMET screens. Control
over attachment points and core preservation is now standard.

2. Latent-Space Molecular Editing (Encode–Perturb–Decode)
Foundational latent models
• VAE (Gómez-Bombarelli 2016) and JT-VAE (2018) defined the latent optimization paradigm [7,5].
Local optimization and MCMC in latent/graph space
• MIMOSA (2020) reframes optimization as MCMC with constrained moves, bridging latent-space smoothness with explicit
structural edits [18].
• Modof (2020) embeds graph-difference learning into a latent model, focusing on single-fragment modifications and controlled
similarity [4].
Scaffold-targeted conditional latent models
• CLaSMO (2024) explicitly conditions a latent CVAE on substructures attached to specific scaffold environments, combining LSBO with scaffold-site selection [12].
• Radinsky’s unpaired molecule translation and CFOM leverage latent representations coupled to translator architectures for property-improving analog generation [25,23].
Trend: Latent models have matured from global property hill-climbers into conditional, scaffold-/substructure-aware editors
that better preserve lead structure, often coupled with Bayesian or MCMC search.

3. MMP/Bioisostere Transformations
Infrastructure and rule extraction
• mmpdb (2018) provides the scalable MMP engine; numerous later methods mine MMPs for local transformation rules [2].
Learned transformation models
• He et al. 2020 is a flagship example: supervised SMILES translation trained on MMP-derived pairs, with explicit multi-property
optimization from a single seed [3].
• Delete (2023) uses MMP-based cuts in one of its masking schemes for linker design and fragment elaboration [28].
• MolEditRL (2025) and MECo (2025) mine MMPs and reaction-derived edits to build realistic editing corpora and edit
vocabularies [46,39].
Ring/bioisostere-specific tools
• Scaffold Keys (Ertl 2021): an explicit web tool for discovering bioisosteric scaffolds via scaffold feature similarity [16].
• These tools are increasingly being fed into generative/editing frameworks rather than used only for analysis.

4. Fragment-Based Recombination / Retrosynthetic Decomposition–Reassembly
Traditional fragment-growing and reaction-driven design
• DOGS (2012) and DOTS (2018) established reaction-based fragment assembly with guaranteed synthetic routes [49,50].
Deep fragment RL and assembly
• Ståhl 2019, FREED 2021, fragRL 2023, and SHARP 2025 progressively refine fragment-based RL:

• Growing/linking optimizers (Descamps et al. 2025): building-block selection and reaction simulation to form new molecules,
emphasizing restrictable chemistry and feasibility [32].
Fragment-based flow models and language models
• InVirtuoGen (2025): discrete-flow over rBRICS fragments, enabling fragment-constrained design and lead optimization
[13].
• Trio / FRAGPT (2025): fragment sequence language models with DPO-aligned QED/SA preferences and MCTS planning [47].
Trend: Fragment and retrosynthetic approaches have shifted from heuristic design-of-experiments around fragments to learned
fragment assembly and RL, with a strong synthesizability-by-construction ethos. Many frameworks now allow starting from a
user-provided core or fragment set to generate analogs.

5. Ring System / Heterocycle Replacement and Scaffold Hopping
Rule- and template-based approaches
• Early medicinal-chemistry workflows (Segall, DOGS, StarDrop) integrated ring modifications and core replacements as
reaction rules [48,49,50,51].
Data-driven scaffold similarity and hopping
• Scaffold Keys (Ertl 2021): a similarity-based method for finding bioisosteric scaffolds, with a public web tool [16].
• Xu et al. 2022 extend scaffold generators to consider 3D shape and chiral pharmacophore for stereospecific scaffold
hopping [44].
Integration into general-purpose generators/editors
• MIMOSA includes ring replacement operations guided by property constraints [18].
• Delete (2023) and SHARP (2025) explicitly benchmark tasks termed “scaffold hopping” as part of a unified fragment/linker/scaffold decoration framework [28,27].
• PathFinder and similar tools support core enumeration (ring/heterocycle replacements) within retrosynthetic constraints [15].
Trend: Ring/bioisostere replacement has morphed from domain knowledge and curated motif lists into algorithmic operators
embedded within larger generative or editing systems, often accompanied by 3D and pharmacophore criteria.

Emerging Patterns and Implications for Future Research
1. From de novo to local conditional editing:
• The field has moved from global generative models to models that accept a specific input (scaffold or molecule)
and propose constrained edits.
• This is essential for practical lead optimization and aligns with your focus on single-molecule input ’ analog set output.
1. Increasing integration of synthesis and feasibility:
• Early models used SA scores; newer ones use reaction templates (PathFinder, REACTOR, growing/linking optimizers) [15,17,32], reagent-level fragment sourcing [24], or synthesizability masks/SEM models [27].
• Future systems are likely to include full retrosynthetic route design in the loop, potentially constrained to vendor-specific spaces like Enamine REAL.
1. Property-aware, multi-objective optimization becomes standard:
• From simple QED or penalized logP to multi-objective docking + QED + SA + ADMET RL and BO frameworks
[8,24,27,12].
• Expect increasing use of multi-property profiles and uncertainty-aware or active-learning-guided loops (e.g.,
PathFinder+FEP+AL [15]).
1. Interpretability and human-in-the-loop workflows:
• Code-level edits (MECo), natural-language instruction editing (MolEditRL), and multi-agent platforms with “Medicinal
Chemist” agents [39,46,42] point toward human-interpretable and auditable systems.
• For use in medicinal chemistry teams, these frameworks will likely dominate over black-box latent-space optimizers.
1. Convergence of method categories:
• Many recent frameworks (Delete, SHARP, Trio, multi-agent systems) blend fragment recombination, scaffold decoration, ring replacement, and local graph edits into a unified action space [28,27,47,42].
• Distinctions between “scaffold decoration” and “fragment-based recombination” are becoming operational rather than
conceptual; what matters is the granularity of edits and constraints.
1. Open-source ecosystems and orchestration frameworks:
• Tools like CReM [1], Deriver [38], Gargoyles [10], fragRL [24], and various VAE/RL models have open implementations.
• Frameworks such as Deriver explicitly support plug-and-play combination of generators and filters, pointing toward
toolchain-level research rather than single models [38].

Takeaways for Designing Your Own Workflow
From this historical trajectory, a robust modern analog-generation pipeline that matches your criteria can:
• Combine multiple operator classes:
• Scaffold-constrained decorators (e.g., scaffold decorator [6], MoLeR [22], EMPIRE [31]) for R-group variation.
• Fragment-based RL or flow models (FREED [8], fragRL [24], SHARP [27], InVirtuoGen [13]) for larger local/global
modifications.
• MMP-/bioisostere-based editors (He et al. [3], Scaffold Keys [16]) for property-conserving local swaps.
• Latent-space substructure editors (CLaSMO [12], Modof [4]) for subtle but flexible modifications.
• Embed explicit synthesizability controls: reaction templates and building blocks (PathFinder [15], REACTOR [17], growing/linking optimizers [32]), or fragment/action masks guided by SEM models [27].
• Use modular orchestration frameworks (Deriver [38], or your own agent-based platform as in [42]) to sequence these
operators, incorporate property and ADMET predictors, and allow human intervention.
The literature shows a clear trend toward such modular, synthesis-aware, property-aware local editing ecosystems, with
multiple public tools now available to implement each operator class.

How do these fit in?
**ML-Based:**
- **LibINVENT** (via REINVENT4): Generative model for library design; modifies molecular decorations while
preserving a scaffold
- **JT-VAE**: Junction-tree variational autoencoder; encodes molecules to a latent space, enabling interpolation and targeted modifications
**Rule-Based:**
- **MMP-DB** (Matched Molecular Pairs): Database-driven approach; applies known medicinal chemistry
transformations (e.g., swap a methyl for a chloro) that have been observed in real drug optimization campaigns
- **Heterocycle Isostere Explorer**: Systematically replaces ring systems with bioisosteric alternatives (ring
systems with similar shape/electrostatics but different chemistry)
- **RECAP** (Retrosynthetic Combinatorial Analysis Procedure): Fragment-based; decomposes molecules at
known retrosynthetic bonds and recombines fragments

Below I’ll place each of your tools into the same conceptual buckets we’ve been using (scaffolddecoration, latentspace editing,
MMP/bioisosteres, fragment/retrosynthetic reassembly, ring replacement), and note how well they match the “single molecule
in ’ analog set out” goal and what they’re good for.

MLbased methods
1. LibINVENT (via REINVENT4)
Where it fits
• Category: Scaffoldconstrained decoration / Rgroup generation; fragmentbased RL when combined with REINVENTstyle
objectives.
• Closest analogs in the corpus:
• ArúsPous scaffold decorator [6] (same highlevel idea: SMILES scaffold with * attachment points ’ decorations).
• SAMOA [36] (scaffoldconstrained SMILES RNN + RL).

• Representation/operator: SMILES; model learns scaffold + attachment pattern ’ full molecule and can be used
as a prior inside REINVENT4.
Interface vs your requirement
• You give:
• A fixed scaffold with explicit attachment points,
• A scoring function (via REINVENT4: docking, QSAR, QED, etc.).
• The system:
• Samples decorated analogs that all retain the scaffold, i.e., structurally related variants by Rgroup changes,
• Uses RL to bias toward highscoring analogs.
So LibINVENT+REINVENT4 is a fairly direct realization of:
“Single scaffold in ’ many Rgroup analogs out, optimized for chosen properties.”
It does not on its own do:
• MMPstyle tiny edits across the whole molecule, or
• Core replacements / ringsystem hops, or
• Explicit retrosynthetic route enumeration.

• In standard use it does not hardenforce vendorlibrary or reactiontemplate constraints; “synthetic realism” comes
from being trained on druglike data and (optionally) restricting fragment vocabularies.

2. JTVAE
Where it fits
• Reference: Jin et al. 2018 [5].
• Category: Latentspace encode–perturb–decode molecular editing; substructureaware.
• Closest analogs: GómezBombarelli VAE [7], Modof [4].
Representation/operator
• Encodes a molecule as:
• A junction tree of substructures (rings, functional groups), and
• The full molecular graph [5].
• Latent vector has a relatively smooth geometry; decoding from it reconstructs a chemically valid molecule assembled from
learned subgraph vocabulary.
Interface vs your requirement
• You:
• Encode a single seed molecule to latent space.
• Sample near that point, or move along propertyimproving directions (e.g., via Bayesian optimization on latent vectors)
[5].
• The model:
• Decodes structurally related analogs, with the “locality” controlled by how far you move in latent space.
So JTVAE fits your “one molecule in ’ analogs out” requirement, but:
• It does not enforce a fixed scaffold. Similarity is statistical—not a hard Murcko lock.
• It has no explicit notion of reactions or fragments from a vendor library, so synthesizability is not guaranteed.
Synthesizability / property control
• Synthesizability:
• Validity is good (decoder enforces grammar of substructures) [5].
• No builtin synthetic accessibility or route enumeration; you would add SA filters or external retrosynthesis.
• Properties:
• The paper demonstrates Bayesian optimization in latent space to improve properties starting from a seed [5].
• You can plug in your own property predictor on latent vectors to guide optimization.
Bottom line: JTVAE is a strong latentspace editor for “softly constrained” analog design and property optimization. In your
toolkit it’s best used as a global / mediumrange exploration module, wrapped with similarity and SA filters, rather than as a
core, synthesisaware operator.

5. RECAP (Retrosynthetic Combinatorial Analysis Procedure)
Where it fits
• Category: Fragmentation scheme underpinning fragment-based recombination / retrosynthetic reassembly.
• Conceptually similar to BRICS/RECAP/RECAPlike schemes widely used in fragment and library design (and in some
generative models’ fragment vocabularies).
Representation/operator
• Provides a set of cleavage rules corresponding to common retrosynthetic disconnections (amide bonds, esters, carbamates, certain heteroatom–carbon single bonds, etc.).
• Applied to a molecule, it:
• Cuts at those bonds to produce synthetically meaningful fragments (synthons).
• These fragments can then be recombined (often via the same bond types) to build virtual libraries.
Interface vs your requirement
On its own, RECAP is not a generator but:
• A fragmentation backbone:
• Given a single molecule, you fragment it at RECAP bonds; then an enumeration or generative method recombines
those fragments (with partners from a fragment pool or buildingblock library).
• In that sense, it underlies:
• Fragment-based recombination tools like CReM [1] (though CReM uses its own contextfragment scheme),
• Reaction-based enumerators that define cut rules aligned to reactions (PathFinderstyle [15]) or rBRICS/RECAPbased fragment LMs (InVirtuoGen [13], Trio [47]).
So in your taxonomy, RECAP itself sits at the “fragmentation / definition of building blocks” layer, enabling:
“Take a lead, decompose it at retrosynthetic bonds ’ use fragments as seeds for recombination or fragment growth.”
Synthesizability / property control
• Synthesizability:
• Because cuts are at retrosynthetically plausible bonds, recombination schemes that mirror these bonds can be closer
to real chemistry, but:
• RECAP alone doesn’t enforce reagent availability or full synthetic routes.
• Properties:
• Neutral; property effects come from how you recombine fragments and what scoring you apply.
Bottom line: RECAP is a ruleset you build on, not a full analog generator. In your system it would define how you cut
a PrexSyn molecule into fragments before passing them to fragment LMs, recombination engines, or reaction-based
enumerators.
